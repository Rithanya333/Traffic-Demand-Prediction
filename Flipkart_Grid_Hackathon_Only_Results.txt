1 Methodology Overview
1.1 Approach
The model is an ensemble of nine diverse regressors trained using 5-fold time-series cross-validation. The pipeline consists of:

1. Data Preprocessing: Timestamp parsing, missing value imputation, outlier cap- ping.
2. Feature Engineering: Extraction of temporal (hour, minute, 15-min slot), har- monic (Fourier), calendar, geographic (geohash decoding, K-Means clustering), in- teraction, and lag features.
3. Target Encoding: Fold-safe smoothed target encoding for high-cardinality cate- gorical variables (geohash, road type, hour, etc.) to prevent data leakage.
4. Model Training: Nine base models (five LightGBM variants, XGBoost, CatBoost, ExtraTrees, HistGradientBoosting) are trained independently on each fold.
5. Ensemble & Calibration: Out-of-fold predictions are combined via Optuna-optimised weights, followed by a 10-seed bagging average (70/30 blend) and mean scaling cal- ibration.

1.2 Feature Engineering Highlights
• Temporal: tmin (minutes since midnight), 15-minute time slots, harmonic sine/cosine up to 3rd order.
• Calendar: day-of-week, weekend flag, day-of-year harmonics.

• Geographic: Latitude/longitude from geohash, 20 K-Means clusters, cluster-hour/cluster-dow interactions.
• Road & Weather: Number of lanes, high-capacity flag, large vehicles allowed, landmarks presence, temperature (median imputation + missing indicator).
• Lag: 48-day demand lag (masked on day 48 to avoid leakage).

1.3 Tools & Libraries
• Data manipulation: pandas, numpy, geohash2
• Machine learning: lightgbm, xgboost, catboost, scikit-learn (ExtraTrees, Hist- GradientBoosting)
• Optimisation: Optuna (for ensemble weights)
• Environment: Google Colab (Python 3)

2 Results & Discussion
The cross-validation results show a clear performance hierarchy among the nine models. The LightGBM variants (especially the base, aggressive, and alternative configurations) consistently achieved very high R² scores around 0.9627-0.9628, with extremely low stan- dard deviations (0.0018). This indicates that gradient boosting with leaf-wise growth and proper regularisation captures the complex traffic demand patterns exceptionally well.
The XGBoost model achieved a mean R² of 0.5876, significantly lower than the Light-
GBM family. This is likely due to its default hyperparameters not being as aggressively tuned for this specific dataset, but it still provides valuable diversity for the ensemble.
The ExtraTrees regressor (mean R²=0.6325) and HistGradientBoosting (mean R²=0.4447)
showed moderate performance. Their lower scores are expected because they are simpler, more randomised models, but they contribute to variance reduction in the final ensemble. CatBoost performed poorly (mean R²=0.1388) with high fold-to-fold variance. This suggests that either its default parameters are not well-suited for this dataset, or that the target encoding provided to it (instead of its native categorical handling) may have
been suboptimal.
   The "light" and "very light" LightGBM configurations (designed for speed and lower capacity) gave moderate R² around 0.53-0.54, as expected. They are included mainly for ensemble diversity.

2.1 Stability Across Folds
The standard deviations of R² across the five folds are extremely low for the top Light- GBM models (0.0018), indicating that the data partitions are homogeneous and the model is stable. The higher variance observed for CatBoost and the light LightGBM variants reflects their sensitivity to the specific training/validation splits.

2.2 Ensemble Implications
Because the best three LightGBM models have very similar, high performance and are trained with different random seeds, their predictions are likely correlated. However, the inclusion of XGBoost, ExtraTrees, and HistGradientBoosting adds diversity. The Optuna weight optimisation will automatically down-weight models that add little value, and the final 70/30 blend with multi-seed bagging further reduces variance. The mean scaling calibration (using the OOF predictions) corrects any systematic bias and brings the final predictions into the [0, 1] range.
Overall, the ensemble is expected to achieve an OOF R² slightly above the best single
model (0.963) while being more robust to overfitting.
